# Findings

## Research Outcome

The mean reversion strategy failed to generate attractive risk-adjusted returns.

Although a weak negative relationship between recent returns and future returns was identified during exploratory analysis, the effect proved difficult to monetize in practice.

---

## What Worked

### Mean Reversion Exists Statistically

The exploratory analysis correctly identified a small mean reversion effect.

Recent losers occasionally outperformed recent winners over short horizons.

---

### Short Holding Periods Emerged Naturally

The strategy exhibited an average holding period of approximately 7 days, consistent with the short-term nature of mean reversion signals.

---

## What Did Not Work

### Risk-Adjusted Performance Was Poor

The strategy achieved a Sharpe ratio of only 0.37.

Performance was substantially weaker than both momentum-based strategies.

---

### Turnover Increased Significantly

The portfolio required frequent rebalancing as short-term losers changed rapidly.

This resulted in turnover nearly three times higher than the momentum strategy.

---

### Drawdowns Remained Severe

The strategy experienced a maximum drawdown of approximately 98%.

Mean reversion provided little protection during prolonged market trends.

---

## Key Insight

A statistically detectable factor does not automatically translate into a profitable trading strategy.

The mean reversion effect observed in cryptocurrency markets appears weaker and less persistent than the momentum effect.

---

## Connection to Earlier Research

This result reinforces the conclusion that cryptocurrency markets are primarily trend-driven rather than mean-reverting.

Momentum factors generated substantially stronger performance than reversal factors.

---

## Research Conclusion

Mean reversion is unlikely to serve as a standalone source of alpha within the cryptocurrency universe.

Additional filters or complementary signals may be required to improve performance.
